Tinolang Manok

Tinolang Manok is a comforting Filipino chicken soup dish made with tender chicken, sautéed vegetables like sayote (chayote), and sometimes green beans, all simmered in a flavorful broth. It’s often seasoned with ginger, onions, and fish sauce, offering a light yet savory taste perfect for any occasion.

Comaparison of Tinola to Other Recipes.

Compared to other Filipino soups like Sinigang or Nilaga, Tinola stands out because of its unique use of ginger as the main flavoring agent, giving it a warm, soothing aroma. Unlike the sourness of Sinigang or the simplicity of Nilaga, Tinola offers a balance of savory, mildly sweet, and aromatic notes that make it both healing and comforting, often served to those feeling under the weather.

Traditional Chicken Tinola

This is the most common version, made with chicken, green papaya, and malunggay or chili leaves. It is seasoned with ginger, garlic, onion, and fish sauce. This version is considered the original and most popular in Filipino households.

Tinola with Sayote

In areas where green papaya is not available, sayote (chayote) is used as a substitute. This gives the soup a slightly milder sweetness compared to papaya but keeps the comforting flavor intact.

Tinola with Upo

Instead of green papaya or sayote, upo (bottle gourd) is added to the soup. Upo gives a soft, delicate texture that absorbs the ginger broth well, making the dish lighter but still filling.

Tinola sa Gata

A Bicol-inspired twist where coconut milk (gata) is added to the ginger broth. This creates a creamier, richer soup with a slight sweetness, blending the warmth of ginger with the creaminess of coconut.

Fish Tinola

Instead of chicken, fish such as tilapia or bangus (milkfish) is used. The broth remains ginger-based but is lighter and has a delicate seafood flavor.

Pork Tinola

Some households use pork ribs or pork belly instead of chicken. This creates a richer and fattier broth while still keeping the signature ginger taste.

Seafood Tinola

Shrimp, crabs, or mussels are added to the ginger broth, giving it a savory and slightly briny flavor. This is common in coastal areas of the Philippines.

Vegan Tinola

For a meat-free version, tofu, mushrooms, or more vegetables (like upo or pechay) are used. It retains the ginger and garlic base, making it a healthy and lighter alternative.

Tinola

Tinola is a Filipino chicken soup dish known for its ginger-based broth. It is both a comfort food and a staple meal that combines simple ingredients into a hearty and healthy dish.

Course Type: Tinola is commonly served as a main course alongside steamed rice.

Cuisine: Tinolang Manok is a Filipino cuisine staple, passed down through generations.

Keyword: The keywords for Tinola is actually "Tinola" but in some provinces it is called "Tola".

Prep Time: The preparation time for Tinola is 15 to 20 minutes for preparing the chicken and chopping the vegetables.

Cooking Time: The cooking time for Tinola is approximately 35 minutes.

Total Time: Cooking Tinola needed 50 minutes from start to finish.

Serving: Tinola serves 4–6 people.

Calories: A serving of Tinola contains around 250–300 calories, depending on ingredients used.

Author: Kashieca Kaye P. Renoballes

Ingredients

  • 1 whole chicken (cut into serving pieces)
  • 1 medium green papaya (or sayote), sliced
  • 1–2 cups malunggay leaves (or chili leaves)
  • 1 thumb-sized ginger, sliced into strips
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce (patis)
  • 5 cups water
  • 2 tbsp cooking oil
  • Pinch of salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. Heat oil in a pot over medium heat. Sauté garlic, onion, and ginger until fragrant.
  2. Add the chicken pieces and cook until lightly browned.
  3. Pour in the fish sauce and stir to coat the chicken.
  4. Add water and bring to a boil. Lower the heat and simmer until chicken is tender (about 25 minutes).
  5. Add the sliced green papaya (or sayote) and continue cooking until vegetables are soft.
  6. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
  7. Add malunggay leaves (or chili leaves) and cook for another 1–2 minutes.
  8. Serve hot with steamed rice.
